# Business Case — Real Estate Sourcing

## Why Real Estate Sourcing Is Operationally Inefficient

Sourcing real estate opportunities is, in practice, a high-volume information problem before it is a decision problem. Common inefficiencies include:

- **Fragmented sources.** Opportunities arrive from many channels (listings, networks, referrals, marketplaces, direct contacts), each with its own format and level of detail.
- **Inconsistent data.** Key information is often incomplete, unstructured, or described in free text rather than standardized fields.
- **Manual processing.** A lot of time goes into reading, cleaning, copying, and reformatting information before any real evaluation can begin.
- **Limited throughput.** Because review is manual, the number of opportunities a person can realistically assess is capped, and comparison across opportunities is hard to keep consistent.
- **Process variability.** Without a repeatable structure, evaluation depends heavily on who is doing it and how much time they have.

The net effect: time is spent on low-value data handling instead of judgment, and worthwhile opportunities can be overlooked simply due to volume.

## Why Automation Can Help

Automation is well suited to the repetitive, structured parts of sourcing:

- **Standardization.** Incoming opportunities can be normalized into a consistent format so they are directly comparable.
- **Speed and consistency.** Routine processing runs the same way every time, without fatigue or drift.
- **Scalability.** A larger volume of opportunities can be processed and pre-organized for review.
- **Focus.** By removing manual data handling, professionals can spend their time on evaluation and decisions rather than preparation.

Automation does not replace judgment here — it removes the friction that prevents judgment from being applied efficiently.

## Who This Could Help

This type of workflow is relevant for anyone who reviews a steady flow of property opportunities, including:

- **Real estate professionals** managing many incoming opportunities
- **Investors** screening deals against specific criteria
- **Brokers** organizing and prioritizing their pipeline
- **Family offices** assessing opportunities as part of a broader portfolio
- **Sourcing teams** that need a consistent, repeatable intake and qualification process

The common need across these users is the same: turn a noisy, high-volume stream of opportunities into a structured, prioritized shortlist that is easy to act on.

## The Value of Structuring Opportunities

Structuring is the foundation that makes everything else possible:

- Opportunities become **comparable** when described with consistent fields.
- A structured record is **searchable, filterable, and reusable**.
- Consistent structure enables **repeatable evaluation** rather than ad-hoc review.
- It creates a clean basis for any downstream qualification or prioritization.

Even without advanced scoring, simply converting messy inputs into a clean, consistent format delivers immediate operational value.

## The Value of Qualification and Prioritization

Once opportunities are structured, qualification adds the next layer of value:

- **Filtering out** what clearly does not fit defined criteria, reducing noise.
- **Prioritizing** what is most relevant so attention goes to the strongest candidates first.
- **Consistency** in how opportunities are assessed, reducing the variability of purely manual review.
- **Efficiency**, because human time is concentrated where it matters most.

The aim is not to make the final decision automatically, but to produce a well-ordered shortlist that makes the human decision faster and better informed.

## Why AI Is Useful — But Should Remain Human-Supervised

AI, and LLMs in particular, are valuable here because much of the relevant information is **unstructured text**. AI can help:

- Interpret and summarize free-text descriptions
- Extract and structure key details into consistent fields
- Support a first-pass assessment against defined criteria

However, AI in this context **should remain human-supervised**, because:

- Real estate decisions carry financial and contextual weight that requires human judgment.
- AI can misinterpret nuance, context, or incomplete information.
- Source data may be inaccurate, and outputs need verification.
- Accountability for any decision must stay with the professional.

The intended model is **AI-assisted, human-decided**: AI accelerates the preparation and pre-qualification, while a person reviews, validates, and decides. This keeps the benefits of automation without delegating judgment to a system that should not be making final calls.
